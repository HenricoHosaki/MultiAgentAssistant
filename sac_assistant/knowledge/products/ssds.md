# SSDs and Storage Catalog

## NVMe SSD [Kingston NV2 1TB]
An affordable PCIe 4.0 NVMe SSD aimed at general use and gaming, offering a solid balance of speed and price for upgrading a laptop or desktop.

- Capacity: 1TB
- Interface: PCIe 4.0 NVMe M.2 (2280)
- Sequential read speed: Up to 3,500 MB/s
- Sequential write speed: Up to 2,100 MB/s
- Form factor: M.2 2280
- Endurance (TBW): 320 TBW
- Price: USD $54.99

## NVMe SSD [Samsung 980 Pro 2TB]
A high-performance PCIe 4.0 NVMe SSD designed for demanding workloads, large game libraries, and fast load times.

- Capacity: 2TB
- Interface: PCIe 4.0 NVMe M.2 (2280)
- Sequential read speed: Up to 7,000 MB/s
- Sequential write speed: Up to 5,100 MB/s
- Form factor: M.2 2280
- Endurance (TBW): 1,200 TBW
- Price: USD $159.99

## Compatibility — SSDs and Storage
- Both SSDs use the PCIe 4.0 NVMe M.2 2280 interface. They are backward compatible with
  PCIe 3.0 M.2 slots, but will run at reduced PCIe 3.0 speeds (up to roughly 3,500 MB/s) on
  older motherboards.
- Installation requires an M.2 slot on the motherboard or a PCIe M.2 adapter card; neither
  drive works with a traditional 2.5" SATA connector.
- Both are compatible with PlayStation 5 as an internal storage expansion, provided a
  compatible heatsink is installed (neither ships with one by default).

## Frequently Asked Questions — SSDs and Storage
- Q: Will these SSDs work in a PCIe 3.0 laptop?
  A: Yes, both are backward compatible, but speeds will be capped at PCIe 3.0 levels (around
  3,500 MB/s) instead of their full PCIe 4.0 potential.
- Q: Do I need a heatsink to install these in a PS5?
  A: Yes, the PS5 requires an M.2 SSD with a heatsink (either built-in or added separately)
  for thermal management. Neither the Kingston NV2 nor the Samsung 980 Pro includes one by
  default.
- Q: What's the practical difference between 320 TBW and 1,200 TBW?
  A: TBW (terabytes written) estimates the drive's write endurance over its lifetime. The
  Samsung 980 Pro's higher TBW rating means it can sustain significantly more total data
  written before reliability concerns increase, which matters more for heavy workloads than
  typical gaming use.
